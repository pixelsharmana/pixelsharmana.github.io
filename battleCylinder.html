Ambush on the Dahoma

Non mortem, sed mutationem timent stulti. Sed si mihi liceret, neutrum patiar.

The air within the production chamber of the perfume shop hung thick and sodden, permeated by a heady bouquet of crushed lilies. Amidst the manufactory stood Relu Casilaril, a nineteen-year-old mage, specialized in the uncommon ice college. She kept watch over an imposing copper vat. Within it, the viscous, obsidian fluid of lunar-lily essence bubbled and underwent a slow and turbulent boiling.

The heat was threatening to overwhelm the delicate process. With a calculated gesture, the young mage wove her mana with precise gestures and incantations, directing a frigid stream of what looked like undulating blue ribbons into the vat until frost precipitated upon the dark roiling surface and the vat alike.

There came Old Marra, approaching with a measured gait. She was a stunted woman, and she bore the hallmarks of her craft and age: skin weathered by time and fingers dyed a vivid viridian by the juices of crushed herbs. She had draped over her raiment a heavy apron of coarse weave that had absorbed the spillages of a myriad distillations. Coming to a halt beside the vast copper vat, she paused in a moment of contemplation, gauging its temperature with the detached precision of a seasoned alchemist.

"Your cooling worked," Marra observed, her voice a sonorous rasp. "The cost of this particular essence is pretty substantial. If the temperature should happen to drift excessively, the scent will sour into a pungent stench, and the entire batch will be rendered ruined and worthless."

While the process neared its conclusion, the liquid presently occupied its most unstable phase; furthermore, a pronounced temperature problem persisted, for a lingering heat clung to the central depths of the vat, stubbornly resisting the cooling influence that had already claimed the liquid's outer reaches.

The labour at last surrendered to its conclusion. Marra, with a gesture of weary satisfaction, smeared her hands against her apron, an idiosyncrasy which surely provided the explanation for both the indelible tint of her fingers and the perennial filth of her raiment.

Marra ascended a spindly ladder, positioning herself precariously above the brim of the vat. She withdrew from a pocket a rod of fine glass and dipped it into the swirling contents. She held it aloft to catch the rising vapours and, after a superficial whiff, her aged face was transformed by a sudden grin of satisfaction. "It is nigh perfect, Relu. You have been of great service once more. In this essence, there is not a single sour note to be detected! Your supervision of the process has been quite flawless yet again."

She laboured for a short duration to skim the dross from the liquid's surface, ensuring it was unmarred by impurities. She applied a meticulous hand to the final stabilization of the volatile essence, sprinkling a minute quantity of pulverized mineral which Relu couldn't identify which quelled the agitated bubbles rising from the depths. Still poised hazardously above the vessel's rim, she directed a searching gaze toward Relu. "You have been a source of unremitting aid to every soul upon this cylinder since your arrival, yet you seek so little in the way of tribute and you claim so meagre a tithe for your many labours. I cannot pretend to grasp the motive behind such selflessness, but..."

Making her descent from the spindly rungs, Old Marra traversed the cluttered laboratory toward a cabinet of many compartments. Her fingers delved into a dark recess. She retrieved a small pouch. Lifting it aloft, you could clearly hear the chinking of coins. "Behold the recompense for your hard work, supplemented by a small gratuity by yours truly. You have been most deserving, of this and much more. Please, take it, and let it serve to purchase some small luxury or finery to delight you, for I sense you have much need for it."

Relu accepted the pouch, yet, performing but a cursory audit of the glittering specimina within, plucked a mere pittance of coins before she proffered the pouch back to her. "Your benevolence, Mistress Marra, borders upon the prodigious," she murmured, "yet this quite surpasses the modest value of my labours."

An impression of profound melancholy beset the ancient woman. It was due to the girl's unfeigned benevolence, for in that very kindness, Marra glimpsed the stark, unyielding nature of her own existence, a quiet, resigned acceptance of her role as a mere beneficiary of the virtues displayed during their occasional gatherings. The realization that she was destined to remain an eternal recipient in their meetings. It was a pervasive sadness, a muted desolation, an accumulation of a truth that eluded the grasp of even the most florid of rhetorics.

The gnawing sensation of unredeemed obligation grew further by Relu’s refusal to depart. Instead, the latter chose to immerse herself in the shop’s drudgery. This unexpected transition to toil provided the old woman with a fresh cause for wonderment. Grasping a rag and a bucket of water, the young maiden proceeded to wipe away the floral residues that clung to the furniture, before turning her attentions to the scrubbing of the floor.

Composure returned to Marra as she observed the maiden, yet beneath her stoic exterior lingered an unexpressed desire to reach out, not merely to bridge, but to reach something inside the wintry spirit of the young woman. Old Marra ventured, her tone carefully modulated to suggest a passing thought as she leaned her weight against the great, heavy vat. "Your sudden departure shall surely provoke much speculation among the neighbours," she began. "The smith’s lad was even wondering if your afternoon might be unburdened... to help him cool his molds, naturally..." she concluded with mischievous levity far more reminiscent of a capricious adolescent than an sexagenarian.

Regrettably, Relu’s visage offered no telltale signifier, no ephemeral flicker of recognition to bridge the gulf between them. Neither the sudden, rosy efflorescence of a blush nor did any slight wrinkling of the nose betray a flicker of disdain for the blacksmith’s apprentice. Not a single hint as to her hidden disposition toward the blacksmith’s son. One was left to ponder whether her subtleties were of so profound a nature as to elude common perception, or perhaps the boy was simply excluded from the calculus of her romantic or intimate desires? Still, there was a peculiar quality to her indifference, a sense that she played by a different, more esoteric set of rules, suggesting a state of being where the familiar passions of the heart held no dominion.

"Hmm... I don't know. It would depend whether the fleeting hours shall deign to permit it. I was planning on venturing toward Nider," Relu explained, pausing to direct her attention toward the window. The glass framed a modest portion of the Danoma cylinder’s inner artificial expanse, showcasing the formidable cyclopean ribs of the superstructure in stark juxtaposition with a nearby woodland, its boughs burdened by a heavy mantle of snow.

As Relu finished cleaning, the workshop underwent a transformation most startling. The atelier's surfaces were now scrubbed of all dross and dust. An aroma of lilies hung suspended in the air. Marra turned her attention back from her shop to Relu. The old woman's features betrayed weariness. There was the of duty yet entwined with it was a sentiment of penance, or possibly even remorse.

"Nider is a destination of no small merit, and while its environs offer a pleasing diversion, the peregrination will be a most exhausting affair," Marra remarked, her fingers kneading her nape. "Since your path will bring you near Nider, would you perhaps deign to perform a singular additional favour? It is a trifle in scale, but a delicate one."

The aged woman approached a shelf to retrieve a minuscule phial of a black substance. This fragile treasure, whose dimensions were scarcely more substantial than a human finger, was then deposited with care into a small, tin-clad box, its interior cushioned by a bed of soft moss.

"This," Marra articulated with gravity, "is but a minuscule specimen of pure essence. It contains a fragment destined for Kaelen of Oros, the village nestled between here and Nider. His purpose is to observe the subtle fermentations that occur when this particular distillation is introduced to his rose oils. A delicate experiment, to be sure. Yet, I must impose to you scrupulous care, for should the glass suffer even an hairline fracture, it would invite the very air itself to mar the purity."

A profound melancholy shadowed her features as her eyes sought the shadows beneath her feet, her voice now a murmur. "I feel myself a sanguineous leech, Relu. You have spent your time cooling the vat since this morning and you've just now scoured the entire workshop, yet here I stand, bestowing upon your transit a further obligation. Since you solicit neither the lustre of coin nor the sweetness of a moment’s respite, this new demand feels a most rapacious imposition upon your benevolence. To demand more of your time feels a gluttonous theft of the most precious resource of all."

With a countenance bowed in most profound obeisance, she presented the small tin box toward Relu. "Should you deign to accept doing this favour," she continued in murmur, "it is my certain belief that Kaelen will find a means of rewarding you that far exceeds my own attempt. Nevertheless, permit me a word of caution. The terrain between these settlements can be uneven, do be careful, lest a sudden stumble bring about the ruin of the precious vial."

With a creaking groan, the shop’s entrance swung wide, ushering in a traveler of gaunt constitution and unkempt aspect. The man was garbed in a somber ensemble leather and trousers of a most frayed condition. He bore the scars of many a dusty trek, which spoke of a life spent in unremitting transit. From a waist-girdle dangled a dented wrench. He smelt of acrid sweat mingled with the sharp, pungent effluvium of low-grade spirits.

The man's eyes, minute and bloodshot, swept over the shop with swiftness and unblinking efficiency. One could discern through his gaze a latent malevolence that spoke of a life lived in lawlessness. Whether he were a brigand, a bandit, or a mere 'perturbed youth', his motive appeared to be a simple thirst for desecration or theft. A desire to shatter the fragile order of the world, to pillage the sacred, and to shout his very existence to a world that ignored his anarchy.

He stood as the quintessential archetype of a rebellion bereft of fundamental purpose, a figure of an aimless revolt, a spirit seeking chaos without a true catalyst or true ambition. The rebel was lacking an identifiable telos. For him, the nature of the wreckage was of no consequence. The singular necessity was the act of ruin itself, a violent assertion of autonomy wrought by his own lawless hands and the primitive impact of his fists.

His roving gaze alighted upon the pair of female figures. The younger of the duo possessed a lustre that commanded his undivided fascination. Her very features seemed plucked from a fever-dream: argent tresses, a visage of porcelain smoothness, and eyes of a violet so vibrant they bordered on the tempestuous. The all was dominated by a mouth of most delectable promise and a pair of roseate lips of such allure they seemed ripe for the plucking. His eyes embarked upon a survey down the contours of her form, lingering with lecherous intent upon the modest swell of her bosom, then tracing the narrowing of her waist, before coming to a rest upon the generous expanse of her hips. An ugly grin blossomed across his face, baring the signs of long-neglected dental hygiene.

His coarse voice broke the silence: "So, you are the much-reputed Ice Witch," remarked the man. He advanced toward the pair with a lurching gait, halting a mere finger’s breadth from Relu. His eyes descended upon her décolletage. "It is whispered in the dim of the end of the cylinder that you can induce tremors in a man without even a fingertip’s touch. One is left to speculate... whether the shadowed crevasses of your anatomy are equally characterized by an unapproachable and crystalline hardness."

He extended a rugged hand with an almost possessive movement to grasp the curvature of her hip in a gesture of most perverse intent.

Indignation escaped Marra’s lips. Seizing a heavy jar of roseate oil, she slammed it onto the counter so hard that the impact resonated through the room like a funeral knell. "Mind your puerile utterances, you sordid pig!" she commanded, her cheeks blooming in a roseate hue. "Lest you forget, this establishment is a sanctum of labour, far removed from the squalid, lightless gutters of the Alferstian slums. show respect to Relu, or I'll pulverize it against your thick brow."

Not with the reflexive startle of a man truly threatened, nor even with a suddenness of motion, did the man withdraw his hand from its precarious position. It was with a mocking expression that he surveyed Marra. He allowed his fingers to linger uncertainty near Relu's hip, hovering there with a calculated insolence.

Old Marra’s face was no longer merely red, it was a mask of pure unadulterated contempt. "You think you're clever, you pathetic, greasy fuck? Pull that shit-crusted hand back right now, you fucking retard. One more inch of that wandering finger and I'll shove this jar so far up your arse you'll be tasting roses for a month!"

Retracting his hand with a sudden motion, a sneer of profound contempt etched itself upon his features. "You have a sharp tongue for a dried prune," he murmured to Marra. Then, directing predatory intent toward Relu, he continued his censure. "And you, petite sorceress, do not harbour the delusion that a few pretty threats will afford you sanctuary. While this shop is a veritable beacon for the criminal, you possess a vulnerability as fragile and yielding as these pale lilies."

With this said, he pivoted and retreated through the egress. Thereupon, the bell gave a trill of unmerited cheer, a simple jolly instrument of copper, intended for the mundane business of greeting merchants and notifying the master of his shop, yet its ringing bore a mordant irony, for it served only to announce the withdrawal of a creature of most unsettling disposition, a man possessed of a predatory ferocity and a mind ravaged by the tumult of a thousand dark impulses.

A quietude as still as one found within a sepulcher permeated the small shop. The vigour that had animated Marra’s stance dissolved into profound dejection. Her shoulders sagged beneath an invisible weight, and her fingers, still tinted green, underwent a rhythmic trepidation against the workbench. As her gaze wandered the cramped confines of her shop, the shadows lurking in the shop’s far recesses appeared to deepen, assuming a more sinister and tenebrous character.

"He is but one among a growing multitude," the old woman breathed, sweeping a hand across her damp brow, "There is a burgeoning influx of vagrants and men whose eyes harbour sinister thoughts. The Danoma has ever been quiet, yet the edges of our isolation are fraying. My sons, far removed now, are of no avail to me should this stranger return with a host of fellows." She paused, her breath hitching as she sought to steady her spirit. "I have weathered many a bitter season, but the universe is becoming bloated with presence. It is growing, most dreadfully, loud."

Relu placed a palm upon Marra’s quivering hand. "Cast aside these needless anxieties, Marra," she murmured, her voice carrying a practised composure, her tone as smooth as polished glass. "That man was a nobody. Men of such mercurial temperament are soon overcome by profound ennui. Such wanderers want spectacle, and they find the monotony of places like this one to be far too pedestrian. They shall inevitably drift away towards more exciting worlds. It is a trifle, a mere lapse upon your peace."

Marra scrutinized Relu's violet eyes, an intense searching that sought a kernel truth. A shuddering uncertain breath escaped her. "It is my fervent hope and desperate desire that your words possess truth, Relu," she murmured. "May your magic hold the encroaching shadows at a distance, just how they ward the stifling heat from the vat."

"I implore you, take this," the old woman murmured, her fingers closed upon the tin box. "Hasten your departure for Oros. The invigorating air of the forest paths shall prove a potent antidote to the nervous agitation you have so graciously borne on my behalf."

Relu offered a nod, ensconcing the small box within a hidden pocket of her cloak before venturing outside into the numbing chill. Before her lay the hamlet of Nomoro, a quaint and bucolic enclave of dwellings fashioned from a haphazard assemblage of well-seasoned timber and plates of metal. The great white shroud of winter was in its final receding stage. In those sun-kissed interstices, the snow had succumbed to a state of muddy dissolution, leaving behind a landscape of sodden earth and bright, slushy pools. In the sheltered and sunless alcoves however, the frost remained deep and formidable. The slow, rhythmic drip of the thaw had fashioned delicate, crystalline icicles that hung from every house and shop.

The Danoma cylinder was of an isolationist character, situated within the Doslaki sub-sector, where various independent enclaves persisted together to ward off the predations of larger space nations. It was here, amidst the small villages, that Relu Casilaril had made her brief, year-long residence. An ice mage, she moved between the hamlets with the quiet purpose of a winter gale.

Her appearance was a study in monochrome and violet. A purple flower was pinned in her white hair. She bore a high-collared neckpiece and a violet-striped corset of white, an off-the-shoulder garment of a sweetheart design that graced her slender, small-bosomed form and emphasized her lithe, narrow waist and the soft curvature of her hips. Her sleeves, great bells of white fabric, swallowed her hands, and her white skirt was a mere trifle of pleated cloth ringed with amethyst scallops. A heavy, pointed drape, embroidered in gold and purple hung from her waist. Her legs were shrouded in white reinforced stockings of a geometric zig-zag design, which terminated high upon the thigh to leave a sliver of pale flesh visible. Though she had dwelt upon the cylinder for less than a year, performing the odd, magical task required by the local populace, her reputation preceded her. The villagers, in their simple affection, dubbed her the "Ice Witch."

Relu looked upward. The terrain itself ascended in a prodigious vertiginous curvature, arching from the periphery to converge in a distant, overhead zenith. There were no horizon. It was a sweeping arc that brought the far side of the world into view, suspended precariously above her like an upside-down heaven. In that remote and inverted province drifted the dark, mossy smudges of arboreal expanses and the minute, scintillating sparks of human habitation, clinging to the inner curve of the cylinder, all of which orbited a central axis of sombre grey. A desiccated lacing of structural cables and pneumatic vents that traversed the heart of the hollowed expanse.

The architecture of the hollowed great cylinder was partitioned into a precise, hexadic arrangement of alternating longitudinal laths: three vast stripes of fertile earth and dense verdure were intercalated with three equally wide bands of what looked like glass. This transparent and durable medium allowed the light of the star to cascade into the hollowed interior in a rhythmic cycle of light and shadow.

The atmosphere bore the dual, clashing notes of rain-drenched pine, and the sterile, metallic breath of the great oxygen-scrubbing engines. Beneath the dim firmament, a few inhabitants of the hamlet moved with purpose. There were those labouring to clear the encroaching snow, while others stood in clusters, lost to the trivialities of discussion. A romantic couple moving in a slow procession drifted past Relu, seemingly unaware of the wider world beyond themselves.

Relu was acutely sensible of the box’s lopsided mass as it rested in her pocket. With a resolute, gait, she traversed to the periphery of the hamlet and into the forest path toward distant Nider. Each step upon the snow produced a sharp crunch beneath her reinforced stockings. The path drew her into a thicket of spruce and pine of stunted forms. Their trunks stood beneath a heavy shroud of fallen snow. Beneath her, the earth had become a treacherous confluence of compacted frost and patches of mud. Old Marra had been right. An oppressive quietude enveloped her trek, broken only by the huff of her breath and the sharp crunch beneath her feet. Behind her, the village had dwindled into a cluster of rooftops.

Breaching the edge of the grove, the terrain unfurled into a vista of agricultural tracts, organized into vast, rectangular domains of fertile earth, each plot demarcated by fences. There, amidst the furrows, machines for the harvest, iron-wrought and heavy, seemingly dreaming of a still distant but approaching summer. Between two stretches of slush, three children were engaged in a bout of puerile amusement. A singular snowball struck one of the child and incited a shrill chorus of laughter that rose briefly above the liquid sloshing of the mire. Relu walked ahead on the road through this small pocket of frolic, her passage as inconsequential to the children as the drifting of a pale, unnoticed cloud. Far above, the farside of the Danoma presented a nebulous tableau. Even so, the cylinder presided over the landscape with a formidable weight and a deceptive majesty that seemed more a product of ancient nature than of metal and machine.

An unruffled tranquillity pervaded her progress. The atmosphere, frigid and tempestuous in its subtle licks, agitated the skin of her visage and the bare surfaces of her thighs with a gelid touch. Where once the agrarian plots had held dominion, there now emerged fractured stone and desiccated thickets. No dwellings broke the horizon now, nor did any creature stir the dust, leaving the domain surrendered to a stagnant silence. At the edge of a small frozen lake, Relu found herself besieged by a boreal zephyr. Scouring the desolate plain of the lake, it lashed her countenance and made her silvered hair dance in the wind.

Though the path surged with rugged temperament, she remained unburdened by the presence of any fellow wanderer. A pervasive barrenness gripped the landscape as she continued. The road, a mere strip of sodden dirt stretched toward a horizon bereft of any forest or farmsteads now. The only significant landmark was a colossal vent of industrial provenance, a massive, metallic protrusion that loomed over the dust with an air of ancient, mechanical indifference and a single tree a few paces away.

The vent manifested as a towering column of ebony with a flared base. It offered a surface of such peculiar flatness that it almost beckoned weary travellers to repose upon it, as one might a bench or some fountain. It was a common part of the Danoma's structural systems. The vent discharged steam in a slow rhythmic dance. Relu, in the act of passing the structure, found herself enveloped in warmth. The heated air caressed the contours of her visage. Unfortunately, the air she inhaled was laden with an industrial pungency. She was now halfway there.

Save for the quiet steam, the surroundings were surrendered to an absolute hush. Ahead, the road wound its way toward Oros and Nider.

Through the hiss of the vent there shattered a voice, loud and guttural, carrying an uncomfortable familiarity. "Lo and behold! If it is not the little Ice Witch, indulging in a leisurely promenade!"

Relu pivoted toward the sound. There, at a distance of roughly twenty meters emerged from the shadows of the vent the man from the shop. He did not approach with any discernible haste. Rather, he adopted a posture of nonchalance, his hands draped upon his belt in close proximity to his wrench.

A certain mordant mockery infused his greeting. His small bloodshot eyes drifted from the features of her face and began a slow and most insolent survey. His gaze meandered down to her upper thighs and back up to the modest swell of her breasts. While his weapon remained unproffered, a tension permeated his posture, betraying an alertness far removed from the casualness of his stance. His parched tongue swept his lips. "A great distance lies between you and the old crone’s shop," he intoned, the words dripping with a mock-paternal gravity. "To traverse in such isolation is a most precarious venture, little mage. One must remain mindful that in these expanses, objects and persons alike are prone to disappear."

The solitude of the first man proved deceptive. From within the shadowed recesses behind the great metallic vent and nearby boulders, four additional figures emerged. They arranged themselves in a circle, interposing themselves between Relu and her two points of egress: the road back to the now distant village was cut by the man wielding the knife, and the one towards Nider by the leader. These new arrivals lacked the first man’s sardonic politeness, presenting instead unvarnished hostility and forgoing any pretense of civility. Their gazes feasted upon her person with a base gluttonous avarice. Their equipment was meager yet threatening, a heavy wooden club, a knife, and two pipes.

"You have wandered far, far indeed, from the safety of the village," remarked the leader. "Moreover, your vestments are far too flamboyant for a solitary stroll through these wastes. One might almost surmise that you court the sudden predations of a stranger, if not the forceful embrace of a roaming brigand..." Between the avarice of flesh and the trepidation of his spirit, indecision settled upon the bandit leader. He entertained a vision of swift subjugation, contemplating the rending of her pristine, snowy vestments and the subsequent disarray of her porcelain flesh. Yet, he remained poised in a state of dubious resolve, wondering if the prospect of such carnal conquest was worth the sudden gelid retribution of a frost-born enchantment.

The marauders advanced with deliberation, constricting their encirclement until the maiden stood ensnared. Gone was the serene hush. In its stead rose heaving breaths and the discordant, screeching friction of metal pipes scraping against the vent.

With a fluid grace, Relu assumed a defensive posture with a deliberation that suggested a profound familiarity with combat magic. Her feet were splayed and firmly rooted, while her arms held in a poised readiness. In a tone of chilling neutrality, she delivered her ultimatum. "I counsel you, sirs, to withdraw immediately, lest I shall be forced to transform your warm blood into brittle shards of ice."

A sudden violence seized the leader's expression. As he reached for the heavy wrench at his hip, he approached her, his eyes a mixture of lust and calculating cruelty. "Should you fail to surrender the phial," he murmured, "we shall drag you into the shadows of the vent, where we may strip that exquisite white corset from your frame and take our turns with you until your voice is spent in a final rattling gasp. Should you attempt to cast any spells, we will start cutting you. We will cut your fingers off and shove them into your pretty mouth, understood?"

The quintet of ruffians advanced with deliberation until but a few paces remained between them and the young mage. He who bore the massive club raised it in a gesture of impending concussion. Conversely, the knife-bearer held his blade in a low, sinister alignment. Simultaneously, the two cohorts armed with iron pipes drifted into a flanking maneuver, intent on besetting the girl from the sides.

With a coarse suddenness, the man behind her clutched a generous bundle of her hair, wrenching her head back in brutal dominance while his blade pressed against her throat. As the sharp point breached her skin, Relu reacted with desperation. Seizing his left wrist with a frantic grip, she pivoted her hips in a singular, sweeping arc, transforming his aggressive velocity into his own undoing. Though she herself suffered a momentary loss of poise and stumbled, the brigand was propelled forward, meeting the earth in a violent impact.

No sooner had he impacted the ground that she pointed a finger at him, completing a spell with her motion. Two shards of jagged ice blossomed from her fingertip and projected themselves with alarming velocity. The first lanced deep into his side, breaching his abdomen, prompting a shuddering intake of breath as the killing cold took hold. The second spike found its mark in his right forearm, striking the brachioradialis with an audible crack. The ice exploded into needles, taking the flesh with it and reducing the muscle and skin to a gruesome confetti of frozen gore. His forearm was pulverized into a slurry of blood and pulp. He let out a howl of singular agony, his surviving hand reaching futilely at his ruined stub, clutching at what had just ceased existing.

Abandoning all patience, the four remaining men descended upon her in a sudden discordant momentum. The brute wielding the club swept it toward her head, a blow so formidable that she recoiled into a desperate crouch to evade it. Simultaneously, the duo of pipe-wielders moved. One sought to harm her ankles in a debilitating strike, while the second endeavoured to pin her left arm against her side. All the while, the leader lunged to seize and slam her with the ultimate purpose of hurling her against the side of the vent.

A brutal impact of metal upon bone marked the moment the pipe struck her tibia. A sharp, burning fire ascended her leg and elicited a jagged cry from Relu. Her left arm, now pinned cruelly against her ribcage by the assailant, became the fulcrum of a sudden gambit. Rather than retreating, she thrust herself forward into the man. The suddenness of her momentum unsettled the man into a backward stumble. Both the other wielder of the pipe and the wrench-bearing leader faltered in a clumsy attempt to avert a collision.

With her right hand, Relu ensnared the arm of the man pinning her, and through that touch, forced a massive amount of mana to freeze his limb. A grotesque metamorphosis ensued: his flesh, once ruddy and vital, assumed a translucent, waxen azure before almost immediately deepening into a lurid, frozen violet. A ragged and high scream escaped him, and a convulsive tremor seized him. His face faded to a ghastly grey, marking the onset of physiological collapse.

With a desperate effort, Relu exerted a forceful pull to extricate her limb from the man. The frozen arm suffered a cleavage far more violent than a simple break. It did not merely fracture, it underwent a total and catastrophic disintegration. Jagged morsels of flesh interspersed with splinters of bone were scattered across the ground. A spray of gore flecked her white corselet leaving a macabre pattern of frozen droplets on her. Where a functioning arm had once been, there now lay only macerated human pulp.

She paid no heed to her slumped enemy now on the ground, nor to the grisly spectacle of gore. Her gaze was fixed instead upon the three survivors. The leader was in the midst of wrath and disorientation. As the man with the heavy club prepared a second, more devastating swing, the third assailant closed the interval toward her wounded leg, intending to conclude the carnage by shattering her tibia with one well-placed blow.

Relu sank into a low crouch, her arms outstretched to unleash a flurry of frigid missiles. The first of these struck the club-bearer at the temple, a blow so forceful it shattered the cranium and sent his eyes reeling in a fit of stupor. The second transfixed the abdomen of the pipe-bearing man, lancing through the viscera and likely violating the diaphragm or the liver. His only sound was wet, gurgling expiration as he crumpled to the earth.

Propelled by an infusion of savage, unbidden energy, the leader lunged, his thick fingers ensnaring her throat before a single further incantation could pass her lips. The force of his onslaught precipitated her violent descent, her head narrowly evading the ferrous base of the vent. Had the impact occurred, her cranium would have been rent asunder and spilt her brain across the very mud in which she now struggled for survival.

The jarring impact sent a concussive tremor that violently shook the inside of skull. A gasp escaped as her lungs were abruptly emptied, and a creeping gloom began to obscure the peripheries of her vision, leaving her world a blurred and dimming landscape. The pressure upon her windpipe attained a formidable intensity. She could feel the frantic pulse of her carotid vessels drumming against the grip of the brute. Subjugated by his heavy body, her chest was flattened and her body entombed in the mud. His thumbs pressed hard into her throat in an effort to shatter her windpipe. The breath of life became a fleeting elusive thing for Relu, whose eyes could behold nothing but the looming, terrible face of her killer.

The brute stooped, his features descending toward hers until the air between them grew heavy with the scent of alcohol and iron. His gaze, wide and feverish with a bloodshot intensity, met hers, while his lips were curled back in a grin of such unhinged mania as to suggest a mind entirely surrendered to the chaos of the struggle.

"You think you won, you little bitch?" he snarled, his voice raspy and loud. "You killed my friends, you are nothing but a dying girl in the dirt! I am going to rip your pretty white hair out by the roots! I will spread your legs and fuck you until you die! And when you are too exhausted to scream, I will take my knife and slice you into strips of flesh! You will be a pile of meat before the sun sets on this cylinder!"

With an inexorable pressure upon her larynx, he consigned her consciousness to pulsing blurs of grey and black. Her arms, rendered impotent by a creeping lethargy, faltered in their feeble resistance against his unyielding assault. Her legs underwent a series of grotesque spasms against the mud. There arose in her gullet an unswallowable accumulation of blood and bile.

Drawing upon the final, flickering embers of her mana, she directed her remaining will into the very fluids of her mouth. With a desperate and precise manipulation of the moisture, she transmuted the viscous saliva into a myriad of infinitesimal ice needles, each far more delicate and fine than a single strand of her argent hair. In a rapid, staccato burst, she expelled these impossibly thin frozen lances that emerged from her lips as a fine deadly spray.

The invisible volley found its mark within the man's left eye. The microscopic needles traversed the cornea and the retina without any resistance. To any observer, the assault would have seemed entirely phantom. There was no discernible laceration, no weeping of blood, nor any visible puncture upon the surface of his eye. Such was the infinitesimal fineness of the frozen shards that they had left the flesh of his face seemingly unblemished by their passage.

However, that mania which had animated the man vanished instantly. The strangling pressure upon her throat slackened as his muscles surrendered. His heavy body collapsed upon her. His weight pressed relentlessly upon her abdomen, stifling her breath. His eyes, though they remained open, were filled with a glassy, vacant desolation; a shallow and ragged respiration continued to stir the air.

A period of uncertainty followed as the exact particulars of her desperate spell remained obscured by a lingering mental fog. As the tides of awareness slowly ebbed back into her psyche, she pushed his chest aside with a tremulous effort, causing him to roll onto his side amidst the mud. Though his breathing had smoothed into a shallow and rhythmic cadence, his body remained possessed by a strange, fragmented motions. His limbs jerked in erratic twitches, and his right arm rose and fell in a repetitive but meaningless cycle. Even his head tilted and snapped at sharp, jarring angles.

His mouth gaped, and his throat issued a pentad of low and monotonous vocalizations. These five distinct tones were repeated in a constant, unswerving rhythm, possessing neither a change in volume nor a variation in pitch. Every few seconds, this identical sequence of sounds was produced anew, a loop that persisted without pause, as if the man had been reduced to a mere mechanical instrument.

Relu's microscopic lances of frost had with most pitiless luck rent the neural filaments of his encephalon. That delicate architecture, which once housed vibrant memories, the tempestuous surges of his passions, and the very rudder of his volition, lay now a tattered and unrecognizable ruin. The formidable adversary who had once cast shadows upon Relu and Marra had vanished into the void of non-being. What endured here was merely a biological detritus, a hollow assemblage of spasmodic muscular tremors and meaningless vocalizations. His eyes, wide and unblinking, remained fixed, failing to track Relu's movements. They possessed no liveness to follow anything more. The spark of intellect had been forever doused. He endured devoid of the quintessence of humanity, a mere biological aberration that mocked the very definition of a living being.

The man’s face had been reduced to a featureless canvas upon which no emotion could be inscribed. The musculature, specifically the orbicularis oculi and the zygomaticus, had collapsed into a state of absolute flaccidity, devoid of the expressions of any mortal plight. There were no squint of suffering, nor widening of the nostrils, nor trace of the spirit’s retreat.

A thick, diaphanous fluid, which Relu identified through grim deduction as the vitreous humour, began to weep from the microscopic apertures of his eye. As it flowed, it merged with the congealed blood of his shoulder injury. Though the light of the cylinder caught the glint of this pooling mix, no flinch accompanied the liquid’s trek into his nostril, nor any shudder as it coated his skin. He resided there as a mere husk of a man. Of all the horrors manifest, none was so singularly revolting as the dissolution of the left eye. As the internal pressures vanished, that vital tension which had hitherto endowed the eye with its firm aspect evaporated. The cornea descended into a state of clouded turbidity. The eye itself, collapsing under the weight of its own desiccation, sank deep into the cavernous socket, reduced to a mere rugose and withered mass of ocular detritus.

Having unmoored herself from the leader’s grasp, Relu struggled to summon a semblance of stability to her trembling legs. Her vision, clouded by the lingering shadows of near-asphyxiation, swept across the clearing. She beheld the macabre.

The ruffian, who but a moment prior had pressed a murderous knife to her jugular, lay unmoving a short distance removed. Having been impaled by those translucent daggers of ice, his abdomen surrendered a thickening pool of sanguine fluid, which crept outward in a slow, obsidian spread to be swallowed by the hungry, indifferent earth. Of his former arm, naught remained but a mangled mix of flesh with bone fragments jutting out.

Within a modest depression of the surrounding sludge lay the club bandit, whose skull had been struck by Relu's ice projectile. From the side of his head there issued a slow, syrupy efflux of blood, which traced a dark rivulet through the grime of his countenance. His respirations were stertorous and labored, possessing that peculiar rattling quality indicative of a profound and catastrophic stupor. Though the spark of vitality persisted, his eyelids remained clamped shut, and his skin was waxy and devoid of bloom.

Yet, if the first two victims offered a study in pallid stillness, the third interposed a much more frantic and unseemly agitation. This wretch, his abdomen breached, appeared to be engaged in a desperate, losing struggle with his own anatomy, his hands submerged deep within the moist, jagged fissure of his abdominal wall. From this cavernous breach, there issued a rhythmic, gurgling sibilance, a sound of air mingling with blood that accompanied the periodic spray of a dark, frothy exudate. Each respiratory effort elicited a wet, gurgling cacophony from the wound, a sound that heralded the intermittent expulsion of a dark, effervescent gore. His visage, a mask of torment, seemed to quiver with the terror of the dying.

Finally, her gaze descended upon the last unfortunate brigand. The one she had froze and ripped his arm away. He was slumping in a posture of wretched defeat against the base of the nearby tree. A trail of drying blood traced the path of his misery through the loam. His complexion, having been stripped of its healthful rose by both the loss of blood and the insidious chill of her frost-magic, presented a most unsettling, translucent ashen hue, and he was beset by a series of violent, rhythmic convulsions. his eyes wandered aimlessly, and he lacked the fortitude even for a cry, yielding instead a weak, rhythmic soughing of breath. The wreckage of his limb lay nearby in the mud, a grotesque mound of pulverised bone and pulped, frozen flesh.

Relu stood poised over the crumpled heap of broken men, her respiration laboured and uneven following the recent exertion, struggling against the scent of gore and the brackish stench of the vent. Her mind was occupied by a singular, adamantine necessity. A sharp imperative that the inhabitants of the Danoma cylinder should never again be subjected to the caprice of such lawless men.

With somber purpose, she approached the man with the cracked skull first. With a deliberate gesture of her hands toward his extremities, she unleashed a translucent ice that slowly crept across his skin, weaving a frigid shroud around his arms and legs. The very flesh of his appendages succumbed to a sudden, brittle desiccation, transmuted into rigid, frost-bitten carrion.

Relu’s attention pivoted toward the one whose abdomen lay rent with a dyspneic cadence. His eyes rolled with a terrified rhythm as she approached. A gurgling murmur of recognition, thick with the ichor of his wounds, bubbled from his throat. And yet, Relu offered no consolation. Instead, she directed a sharp sub-zero discharge at his arm and legs. The frost petrified the bloody integument. The sensation of an encroaching frost licked through his vasculature, lulling his viscera into a deathly numbness, stilling his pulsing organs. His lamentations were abruptly extinguished.

With a final, somber movement, she turned her countenance toward the tree, toward the last bandit, whose frame was besieged by the convulsive tremors of a terminal shock. "Stop! Gods, just stop!" he shrieked, his voice cracking into a frantic, high-pitched sob. "It was just a- a bit of a scrap! You don't have to... you don't have to butcher us like cattle!"

With a grim resolve, she laid her hands upon his trembling legs, pouring a great amount of frost into him. He spat a mouthful of blood toward her, his face a hateful grimace. "You're a monster! A cold-hearted butcher! Look at him! You've turned him into a statue! Don't leave me a useless, limping wreck! Curse you, you fucking bitch! Curse your very soul for this!"

In a singular moment, the agitation ceased, replaced by an immutable stasis; the flesh, now bleached to a ghostly, alabaster white, hardened into a gargantuan, frozen hunk of integument, as motionless and cold as a relic plucked from a forgotten glacier. "Please... not my legs," he whimpered, his remaining limb jerking in violent, involuntary spasms. "I'll never... walk... I yield! We yield! Just... just leave the rest of me alone!"

Wordlessly, she then put her hands on his arms. As the blue tint began to creep up his arm, a raw animalistic scream tore from his throat. "You're a devil in a woman's skin! We only wanted the coin, you butcher! You're destroying our very bodies! Fuck you! May you rot in the very ice you use to break us! No! No, no, no!" He tried to scramble backward, his frozen legs slipping in the bloody slush. "It's too cold! It's biting me! Stop the frost, damn you! Stop it! Not the frost. Not the cold. Just... let me stay whole..."

She remained unperturbed by his lachrymose lamentations and his frantic, hollow denials and disregarded his spasmodic cries of agony and the desperate, puerile protests. Though three wretches had escaped the finality of death, their humanity had been systematically stripped away, leaving naught but pulpy masses of macerated tissue, shorn of the agency to strike or the agility to traverse. It was a chillingly calculated mutilation. A cold methodical deconstruction of the flesh that left them as nothing more than breathing, stagnant lumps of meat, trapped in perpetual disability. In this clearing, now a graveyard of pulverized limbs, she stood as the singular arbiter of motion, the only creature yet unmarred by the stillness of the cold.

The surge of vigor which but a moment prior had animated her began its slow recession, bequeathing to her limbs a leaden malaise and a most unseemly tremor of the fingers. Her gaze fell, with a certain melancholic detachment, upon her white corset, now a canvas of grisly tableau: dark, drying rivulets of crimson from the unfortunate man, whose arm had been rendered into a pulverulent mess, intermingled with mud that besieged her sweetheart neckline. With a weary gesture, she employed her bell sleeve to sweep dab it all away.

Another layer of mud clung stubbornly to the lower edges of her pleated white skirt, while her stockings, once of a pristine alabaster, now presented a grotesque mix of blood and mud. Relu tugged the fabric downward, seeking to drape it once more over her hips. As She labored to smooth her disarrayed garments, she discovered to her profound chagrin that the white floral accessory which had adorned her tresses had been lost to the chaos, presumably crushed by a heavy and indifferent boot. This loss weighed heavily upon her. The flower had been a precious memento, gifted to her by the son of the local blacksmith.

A nervous adjustment followed as she pulled the white fabric of her neckpiece tight against the throat, a desperate attempt to conceal the bruised impressions left by her strangling. Even as her long, argent locks lay disheveled, she applied a certain desperate artistry to the smoothing of her matted tresses.

With a stride far from the graceful, she retreated from that grim cairn of frost-bitten and rent flesh. Each time her left foot made purchase upon the ground, a flash of agony ascended from her tibia to the very pivot of her hip, as though a lance of white heat had been driven through the marrow. The initial blinding stabs of the impact had begun to subside into a deep throb, suggesting a hairline fissure within the bone itself. Her chest still bore the dull, throbbing protest of the brutal percussion of her fall against the ground.

Relu turned her attention to her cargo, her hand drifting to the pocket of her cloak to ensure the tin vessel’s integrity. To better observe the contents of the tin vessel, she lowered herself with a grimace, the movement exerting a painful pressure upon her leg. Her cold fingers fumbled with the lid to reveal an interior lined with moss, once a pale and desiccated cushion for the fragile vial, but now transformed into a dark and sodden mass.

The vial sat broken. Its sharp clear teeth were biting into the verdant moss beneath the cylinder’s dying light. The lunar-lily essence, that costly and ethereal nectar of Marra’s long-suffering industry, had bled into the padding of its container. it released a floral aroma so cloying and pervasive that it stood in grotesque opposition to the heavy stench of the dying. The sample was lost, transformed into a dark wet smear of what might have been a magnificent thing. It was now only a wasted promise.

The rhythmic, clockwork moaning of the lobotomized man and the liquescent gasps of his companions dissolved into an indistinct cacophony. She cast a backward glance toward the village, contemplating the very trail which, earlier, had presented an image of undisturbed peace. A tedious and agonizing peregrination awaited her, for every stride upon her fractured tibia would precipitate a sharp, jarring resurgence of the combat’s memory. The cloying mud-caked weight of her blood-streaked raiment already imposed a most oppressive burden upon her musculature.

Still, a faint, wintry smile played upon her lips. The perfume shop was destined for a period of exquisite stillness, for it signalled the cessation of those unwelcome visitations by unkempt ruffians whose predilections for her hips were matched only by the crude offense of their threats. Old Marra would possess her tranquillity at last. It was a small matter of domesticity to excise the gore of her vanquished foes from the entryway, lest the blood of the unworthy mar the aesthetic of her doorstep.

With an air of profound exhaustion, Relu began the slow, limping trek back through the shadows of the Danoma cylinder.
